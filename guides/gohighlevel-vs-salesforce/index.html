What Salesforce Does Well

Salesforce is not a bad platform. It is an extraordinary platform built for a specific audience. Understanding where it excels explains exactly why it is wrong for most small businesses.

Enterprise-Grade CRM Architecture. Custom objects, custom fields, record types, validation rules, and lookup relationships let you model virtually any business process. A pharmaceutical company tracking drug trials, a manufacturing firm managing distributor hierarchies — Salesforce handles it because the platform was architected for infinite customization. For a solo founder tracking 200 contacts, this is buying a commercial kitchen to make toast.

The AppExchange Ecosystem. Over 7,000 integrations and add-ons. CPQ, contract management, AI-driven lead scoring — if an enterprise function exists, there is an app for it. The catch: most apps cost $10-$100+ per user per month on top of your Salesforce license.

Reporting and Analytics. Custom report types joining data across any combination of objects. Dashboards with drill-down. Einstein AI forecasting. A VP of Sales at a 200-person company can see pipeline by stage, win rate by rep, and forecast accuracy by quarter in real time. This depth justifies the price — for companies that actually need it.

Compliance and Security. SOC 1, SOC 2, ISO 27001, HIPAA, FedRAMP, PCI DSS. For regulated industries, these certifications are not optional — they are procurement requirements. Salesforce checks every box.

Multi-Team Hierarchy. Role hierarchies, permission sets, territory management, opportunity splits, multi-level approval chains. None of this matters for a team of five. For a company with 80 reps across four regions, it is a requirement.

What Salesforce Gets Wrong for Small Businesses

The Pricing Punishes Small Teams

Salesforce charges per user, per month, billed annually:

  • Starter Suite: $25/user/month — basics only, no automation, no custom reports
  • Professional: $80/user/month — adds automation and forecasting
  • Enterprise: $165/user/month — where Salesforce becomes genuinely powerful
  • Unlimited: $330/user/month — full platform including AI and premier support

A solo founder on Professional pays $80/month for a CRM and nothing else. No email marketing. No SMS. No landing pages. No scheduling. No review management. Each capability requires an AppExchange add-on or separate tool. A 5-person team on Enterprise pays $825/month for the CRM alone — before adding Pardot ($1,250/month for marketing automation), storage overages, and AppExchange apps.

You Need an Admin to Make It Work

Salesforce is not a sign-up-and-go tool. Page layouts, fields, Flows, reports, permissions, data imports — all of it requires configuration. Most small businesses hire a consultant ($150-$300/hour) for initial implementation, which typically runs $6,000-$30,000. After that, someone needs to maintain it. The average Salesforce Administrator salary is $75,000-$95,000/year. Enterprise companies budget for this. Solo founders do not.

The Learning Curve Is Brutal

Salesforce's interface was designed for power users who live inside the platform eight hours a day. The navigation structure has hundreds of setup options. The Flow builder uses branching logic and fault paths. The report builder has groupings, cross-filters, and bucket columns. Every one of these interfaces assumes you have training or a Trailhead certification.

A solo founder who signs up and tries to build a basic lead capture workflow will spend a full day Googling things like "how to create a Salesforce flow trigger" and "difference between Process Builder and Flow" (answer: Process Builder is being retired but half the tutorials online still reference it). By day three, most people are questioning their life choices.

No Built-In Marketing Tools

Salesforce manages contacts, accounts, and pipeline. It does not send email campaigns, SMS messages, or build landing pages. It does not schedule appointments or manage Google reviews. For those, you buy Marketing Cloud ($1,250+/month) or stitch together Mailchimp ($30/mo) plus Twilio ($25/mo) plus Leadpages ($37/mo) plus Calendly ($12/mo) plus a review tool ($100/mo). That is $284/month in add-ons before you count Zapier to connect them all.

The Hidden Cost of Salesforce

  • Implementation consulting: $5,000-$30,000. A basic setup with pipeline, field mapping, data import, and three workflows typically runs $8,000-$15,000.
