Step 1: Domain Verification and Sending Configuration

Before you write a single email, you need to authenticate your sending domain. Skip this and your emails land in spam.

Setting Up SPF, DKIM, and DMARC

Inside your GoHighLevel sub-account, navigate to Settings and then Email Services. Click "Add Domain" and enter your business domain. GHL generates three DNS records you need to add through your domain registrar.

SPF (Sender Policy Framework) tells receiving mail servers that GHL is authorized to send on behalf of your domain. You add a TXT record listing GHL's mail servers as approved senders.

DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail) adds a cryptographic signature to every email. The receiving server checks this against a public key in your DNS to verify the email was not altered in transit. GHL generates the record — you copy it into your DNS.

DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting, and Conformance) ties SPF and DKIM together. Start with v=DMARC1; p=none; for monitoring, then tighten to p=quarantine or p=reject once you are confident in your setup.

Add all three through your registrar — GoDaddy, Cloudflare, Namecheap, whatever you use. Verification completes within 24 to 48 hours. GHL shows a green checkmark next to each record once validated. Do not send until all three are verified.

Choosing Your Sending Provider

GHL offers two options: LC Email (built-in, ~$0.001 per email) and Mailgun (more infrastructure control). For most solo founders, LC Email is the right starting choice. You can switch later without rebuilding anything.

Warm-Up Strategy

New sending domains have no reputation. Sending 5,000 emails on day one lands you in spam. Build volume gradually:

  • Week 1: 20-50 emails per day to your most engaged contacts
  • Week 2: 50-100 per day
  • Week 3: 100-250 per day
  • Week 4: 250-500 per day
  • Week 5+: Scale to full volume

Send to people who will open and reply during warm-up. Every engagement signal builds your domain reputation. Every bounce and spam complaint erodes it.

Step 2: The Email Builder

Templates

GHL provides pre-built templates organized by category: welcome emails, promotions, reminders, newsletters. The library is smaller than Mailchimp or ActiveCampaign — dozens rather than hundreds. For most solo founders this does not matter. You will customize heavily, and a clean starting template gets you there faster than browsing 200 options. You can also save your own emails as reusable templates, building a custom library tailored to your brand over time.

The Drag-and-Drop Editor

The editor uses a block-based system: text, images, buttons, dividers, columns, video embeds, and social links. It covers the basics well. What you will not find: custom CSS injection, conditional content blocks, or pixel-level spacing control. If you send primarily text-based emails with a logo, headline, body copy, and CTA button — which most solo founders should — the editor handles it without friction.

Merge Fields and Personalization

GHL calls these "Custom Values." The basics: {{contact.first_name}}, {{contact.last_name}}, {{contact.email}}. You can also pull any custom field — {{contact.company_name}}, {{contact.industry}}, or whatever you have built into your CRM.

Always set fallback values. If a first name field is empty, "Hey {{contact.first_name}}" renders as "Hey " — a blank space that screams mass email. Use "Hey {{contact.first_name | there}}" or write openers that do not depend on the name field.

Step 3: Types of Emails in GoHighLevel

Broadcast Campaigns

One-time sends to a filtered segment. Use for launches, promotions, or newsletters. Select your audience by tags, lists, pipeline stage, or custom fields, schedule the time, and GHL delivers it with open, click, and bounce tracking.

Drip Sequences (Workflow-Triggered)

Automated emails that fire based on triggers and timing in GHL's Workflow builder. A contact submits a form — email one fires. Two days later, email two sends. If they click a link, they get one follow-up. If not, they get a different one. These sequences nurture leads while you do client work.

The all-in-one advantage: in GHL, the same workflow can send an email, send a text, update a pipeline stage, assign a task, or trigger a voicemail drop. Your email sequence is woven into your entire business operation, not isolated in a separate tool.

Transactional Emails

Booking confirmations, appointment reminders, invoice notifications. Handled through Workflows with instant triggers. Keep them short and functional — confirm the time, include the link, tell them what to prepare.

Step 4: Building Your First Drip Sequence

Here is a walkthrough for a 3-email welcome sequence that fires when a new lead opts in.

Navigate to Automation then Workflows in your GoHighLevel account. Create a new workflow from scratch.

Trigger: Form Submitted. Select the form or landing page. When a contact fills it out, the workflow begins.

Action 1: Send Welcome Email. Goes out immediately. Deliver whatever you promised — a guide, checklist, or video — and set expectations for what comes next. Keep it under 200 words.

Wait Step: 2 days.

Action 2: Send Value Email. Genuine value, no ask. A useful insight, mini case study, or common mistake to avoid. Build trust and demonstrate expertise.

Wait Step: 2 days.

Condition Check: Did they click a link in Email 2? Add an If/Else condition to branch the sequence.

If yes — Email 3A: Invitation to Book. They engaged. Invite them to book a discovery call with your calendar link. Direct, clear, no pressure.

If no — Email 3B: Social Proof. Lead with a case study or testimonial. End with the same calendar link, framed around results rather than process.

That is five workflow steps producing a branching 3-email sequence that runs entirely on autopilot. Build it once. Every new lead that enters your funnel receives this sequence without you touching anything. And because this is GHL, you can add SMS steps, pipeline updates, or task assignments to the same workflow — something a standalone email tool cannot do natively.

Step 5: Segmentation

Sending the same email to your entire list trains people to ignore you. GHL gives you three segmentation methods.

Tags. The simplest tool. Apply them through forms, workflows, or manually. Examples: "lead-magnet-downloaded", "discovery-call-booked", "client-active". Use tags to filter broadcast recipients and as workflow triggers. Name them consistently from day one — a messy tag system becomes unusable fast.

Custom Fields. Store structured data: industry, company size, revenue range, service interest. Unlike binary tags, custom fields hold specific values for segments like "industry equals SaaS" or "revenue greater than $50K." Build your key fields before collecting leads.

Pipeline Stage. The method most founders underutilize. Contacts in "New Lead" should receive different emails than those in "Proposal Sent" or "Won." GHL triggers workflows and filters campaigns by pipeline stage, keeping emails relevant to where each contact actually sits in your sales process.

Cold Email vs. Warm Email: Different Tools for Different Jobs

Warm email goes to people who opted in — form fills, clients, subscribers. This is what GHL is built for: nurture sequences, broadcasts, reminders, onboarding.

Cold email goes to people who have never heard of you. Running cold outreach through the same domain you use for warm email risks poisoning your domain reputation. High bounce rates and spam complaints from cold lists drag down deliverability for everything — including your warm sequences to actual clients.

The smart setup: use GHL for everything warm and a dedicated tool like Instantly for cold prospecting. Instantly manages separate sending domains, handles inbox rotation, automates warm-up across multiple mailboxes, and keeps your cold outreach completely isolated from your business domain. When a cold prospect replies and expresses interest, you move them into GHL as a warm lead and your nurture system takes over from there.

This separation is not optional if you are doing both warm and cold outreach at any meaningful volume. It protects your primary domain, keeps your warm email deliverability high, and lets each tool do what it was specifically designed to do.

  • Unlimited contacts. No per-contact pricing. ActiveCampaign at 10,000 contacts costs $159+/month for email alone.

  • Unified contact timeline. Every interaction in one record without switching tools.

What You Lose

  • Fewer templates. Dozens vs. hundreds in ActiveCampaign or Mailchimp.
  • Less email analytics. No deliverability breakdown by provider, no predictive send-time optimization, no engagement scoring.
  • No conditional content blocks. You build separate emails for each segment variation.
  • Less mature A/B testing. Subject line testing only — no split-testing of entire automation paths.

If email is your entire business, a dedicated platform is better. If email is one channel alongside SMS, CRM, scheduling, and funnels, GHL does email well enough while eliminating four or five other subscriptions.

Deliverability Tips

  • Warm up slowly. Follow the schedule above. Gradual increases build reputation; sudden spikes destroy it.
  • Clean your list. Remove contacts who have not opened in 90 days. Remove hard bounces immediately. A smaller engaged list outperforms a large dead one.
  • Avoid spam triggers in subject lines. "Free," "Act now," "Guaranteed" — these raise your spam score. Write like a person, not a promotion.
  • Use a real reply-to address. Replies signal legitimacy. A no-reply address removes one of your strongest deliverability signals.
  • Watch your text-to-image ratio. Image-heavy emails get flagged. Lead with text.
  • Send consistently. One email per week on schedule beats erratic bursts.
  • Authenticate everything. SPF, DKIM, DMARC — all three verified. Non-negotiable.
